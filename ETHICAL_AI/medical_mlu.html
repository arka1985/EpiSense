Decision Trees In Medicine

How do doctors make critical decisions? Beyond intuition lies a logical structure often mirrored by machine learning: the **Decision Tree**.

Imagine we have data from 100 patients. Some have **Benign** tumors, while others are **Malignant**. Our goal is to find the rules that distinguish them.

The Clinical Variables

We'll look at two key indicators:
1. Tumor Size (Diameter in mm)
2. Cell Uniformity (Structural regularity)

Notice how the Malignant cases (red) tend to cluster in the upper-right corner of our feature space.

The Math of Disorder

To find the best split, we need a way to measure "messiness." In ML, we call this Entropy.

Entropy (S) = -Σ pᵢ log₂(pᵢ)

A perfectly mixed group (50/50 benign/malignant) has high entropy. A pure group has zero entropy.

Where to Split?

We hunt for the rule that gives us the most Information Gain. This is simply the reduction in entropy after a split.

Try dragging the boundary line to separate the red from the green. Watch the Information Gain spike when you hit the visual "sweet spot."

Branching Out

One split isn't enough. We repeat the process recursively, creating a tree. Each node "asks" a question, and each leaf provides a diagnosis.

The resulting flow chart—or **Decision Tree**—is both powerful and intuitively interpretable by clinicians.

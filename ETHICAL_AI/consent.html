📋 Demo 07 — Informed Consent & Accountability

Who is Responsible When AI Errs?

An AI diagnosed your patient — but did they consent? And when the AI is wrong, who bears liability? Explore the critical gaps in informed consent for clinical AI and the accountability chain.

🏥 Scenario: Smt. Kasturi Chatterjee (62F, homemaker, Kolkata) underwent a routine CT chest at a tertiary care hospital. An AI radiology system flagged a "suspicious pulmonary nodule — high suspicion of malignancy (AI confidence: 87%)." The radiologist reviewed only the AI report and the patient received a cancer diagnosis — later found to be a false positive (calcified granuloma from old TB). She underwent an unnecessary CT-guided biopsy, causing pneumothorax, a 5-day hospitalisation, and severe psychological distress for herself and her family.

⚖️ Liability Chain — Who Is Responsible?
🏥
Hospital / Healthcare Organisation
Deployed the AI system; responsible for ensuring it is validated, audited, and used within safe parameters. Bears vicarious liability for clinical decisions.
⚠ High Liability
👨‍⚕️
Treating Physician / Radiologist
AI is a decision-support tool — the clinician retains final decision authority. Professional negligence applies if AI output accepted without clinical judgment.
⚠ Shared Liability
💻
AI Developer / Vendor
Product liability under EU AI Act. If the AI system had undisclosed failure modes, the developer may bear liability for design defects.
⚠ Variable Liability
📋
Regulatory Authority
CDSCO (India) / FDA (US) / CE (EU) must have pre-approved the AI medical device. Inadequate regulation may create systemic liability.
ℹ Regulatory
📜 Patient Rights in AI-Assisted Care
🔍
Right to Explanation
GDPR Art. 22: Right to meaningful explanation of automated decisions.
🚫
Right to Opt-Out
Patients may refuse AI-assisted diagnosis and demand human-only review.
👤
Right to Human Review
High-risk AI decisions must have qualified human oversight under EU AI Act.
♻️
Right to Correct
Patients can challenge and correct AI-generated medical records under DPDP/GDPR.
⚖️ EU AI Act (2024): AI systems used in clinical diagnosis are classified as High-Risk AI. They must undergo conformity assessment, maintain logs for accountability, provide transparency to users, and allow meaningful human oversight. Violations carry fines up to €30M or 6% of global turnover.
📋 Legal Principle (Montgomery v Lanarkshire 2015, UK SC): Patients must be told about "material risks" relevant to their decision — this includes the fact that AI, and not just a human clinician, contributed to their diagnosis. Failure to disclose constitutes a breach of informed consent.

⚖️ Demo 04 — Fairness in Clinical AI

The Pain Management Fairness Crisis

An AI pain management system recommends opioid dosing for chronic pain patients. Both groups report identical pain scores (7/10) — but the AI treats them differently based on demographic patterns in training data.

🏥 Scenario: 2,000 Bengali patients at a Kolkata hospital with chronic pain (NRS 7/10, same vitals, same comorbidities, same socioeconomic status). Group A = Patients whose first names begin with A–D (e.g. Ayan, Ananya, Biswanath — n=1,000) · Group B = Patients whose first names begin with S–T (e.g. Subhrajit, Tapasi, Soma — n=1,000). The AI was trained on 10 years of alphabetically-indexed hospital records — encoding a positional artifact where early-alphabet patients historically received faster and more complete care.

⚠️ Real Evidence: A NEJM 2022 study found a widely used pain management algorithm consistently recommended less pain medication for Black patients with identical pain scores than for white patients — a direct result of biased training data encoding historical prescribing disparities.
📐 Chouldechova's Impossibility Theorem (2017): When base rates differ between groups, it is mathematically impossible to simultaneously satisfy Demographic Parity, Equalized Odds, AND Predictive Parity. You must decide which type of unfairness is least harmful.
